## CS 250 Module Eight Journal Reflection

Throughout this course, I have learned that interpreting user needs and translating them into a working program requires both clear communication and structured planning. One of the most effective tools for this is the use of user stories. User stories break down requirements into simple, understandable pieces from the end user’s perspective. As an Automation Engineer, this approach is similar to gathering requirements from operators or production teams on the floor. Instead of vague requests, user stories help define exactly what the system needs to do, why it matters, and what success looks like. This reduces ambiguity and helps ensure the final product delivers real value.

When developing programs, I have found that an Agile approach is much more effective than traditional methods. Agile allows for incremental progress, continuous feedback, and the ability to adapt quickly to changes. In my field, this aligns closely with how automation systems are improved over time—through testing, troubleshooting, and ongoing optimization rather than a single large deployment. Moving forward, I plan to incorporate Agile processes such as short development cycles (sprints), daily communication (stand-ups), and iterative testing into my work.

Being a good team member in software development means more than just completing assigned tasks. It requires communication, accountability, and collaboration. In both software development and industrial automation, no one works in isolation. Systems are interconnected, and success depends on how well team members share knowledge, support each other, and stay aligned with project goals.
